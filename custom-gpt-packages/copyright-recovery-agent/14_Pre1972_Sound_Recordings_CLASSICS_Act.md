# Pre-1972 Sound Recordings & The CLASSICS Act

## Overview

This document explains the unique legal status of sound recordings fixed before February 15, 1972, and how the Music Modernization Act (MMA) of 2018 altered their protection and termination rights.

## Key Concepts

Unlike musical compositions, sound recordings were not granted federal copyright protection until 1972. They were previously governed by a patchwork of state laws. The CLASSICS Act brought them under the federal umbrella with specific timelines.

## Detailed Information

### The CLASSICS Act (Title II of the MMA)
The Compensating Legacy Artists for their Songs, Service, and Important Contributions to Society (CLASSICS) Act extended federal remedies for unauthorized use to pre-1972 sound recordings.

### Tiered Expiration Timelines
The Act established a tiered system for when these recordings enter the public domain, effectively acting as a substitute for traditional termination rights in some contexts:
*   **Recordings published before 1923:** Entered the public domain on January 1, 2022.
*   **Recordings published 1923–1946:** Protected for a 100-year term.
*   **Recordings published 1947–1956:** Protected for a 110-year term.
*   **Recordings published 1957–Feb 15, 1972:** Protected until February 15, 2067.

### Implications for Catalog Owners
For estates holding master recordings from the mid-century era (e.g., 1960s jazz or R&B masters), the 2067 date provides a long runway for exploitation. However, the interaction between these new federal terms and state-level grants can be complex. Furthermore, the MMA established that noncommercial uses of pre-1972 recordings that are not being commercially exploited are permitted under certain conditions.

## Practical Applications

When analyzing a catalog that includes master recordings (not just publishing) from the 1960s, the agent will clarify that these masters are protected until 2067 under the CLASSICS Act, ensuring the estate understands the long-term value of the asset.

## Escalation Requirements

Escalate to specialist counsel if a dispute arises regarding the commercial exploitation status of a pre-1972 recording under the MMA's noncommercial use exception, as this requires specific factual investigation.
