# Foreign Rights Reversion

## Overview

This document details the mechanisms for recapturing copyrights outside the United States. US termination rights (under § 203 and § 304) generally only terminate US rights. Recapturing global rights requires leveraging territory-specific reversion laws.

## Key Concepts

The principle of territoriality governs international copyright. However, recent developments (like the Fifth Circuit's *Vetter v. Resnik* ruling) and new international directives are shifting the landscape, making foreign reversion a critical component of catalog recovery.

## Detailed Information

### The Territorial Limits of US Termination
Historically, US termination only affected the US territory. Section 304(c)(6)(E) states termination 'in no way affects rights arising under any other Federal, State, or foreign laws.' However, the January 2026 Fifth Circuit ruling in *Vetter v. Resnik* held that U.S. copyright termination and renewal can reach worldwide rights. While controversial and potentially subject to Supreme Court review, this is a powerful new tool in negotiations.

### Territory-Specific Reversion Laws
*   **EU DSM Directive (Article 22):** Provides a 'right of revocation' allowing authors and performers to revoke exclusive licenses if their works are not being adequately exploited. Implementation varies by member state.
*   **Canada (Section 14(1)):** Provides an automatic posthumous reversion. Copyright automatically reverts to the author's estate **25 years** after the author's death. Any prior assignment becomes void.
*   **United Kingdom:** Under the Copyright Act 1911, works created between 1911 and 1957 are subject to an automatic reversion 25 years after the author's death. Modern UK law relies more on contractual reversion clauses negotiated in publishing deals.

### Strategic Value
Recapturing only foreign rights (even if US rights remain encumbered by a Work Made for Hire claim) can be highly lucrative and forces publishers to the negotiating table.

## Practical Applications

When advising a Canadian heir whose songwriter parent died in 2000, the agent will immediately flag that under Section 14(1), all Canadian copyrights automatically reverted to the estate in 2025, and the current publisher has been collecting royalties unlawfully since that date.

## Escalation Requirements

Escalate to international copyright counsel if a publisher relies on the traditional interpretation of territoriality to reject a worldwide termination claim based on *Vetter v. Resnik*, as this requires sophisticated cross-border litigation strategy.
