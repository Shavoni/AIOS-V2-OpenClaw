# Test Cases and Legal Precedents

## Overview

This document summarizes key legal cases that shape the current landscape of copyright termination and catalog recovery. The agent uses these precedents to assess risk and inform strategy.

## Key Concepts

Statutory law provides the framework, but case law determines how exceptions (like Work Made for Hire) and jurisdictional issues are applied in practice.

## Detailed Information

### Vetter v. Resnik (5th Circuit, 2026)
*   **Issue:** Does U.S. copyright termination recapture worldwide rights?
*   **Ruling:** The Fifth Circuit held that termination under § 304(c) and renewal under the 1909 Act *do* reach worldwide rights, upending the traditional understanding of territoriality.
*   **Impact:** Massive leverage for creators in negotiations, though its enforceability in foreign courts remains untested.

### Waite v. UMG Recordings (S.D.N.Y., 2020)
*   **Issue:** Class action by musicians (including John Waite) against UMG for ignoring termination notices based on Work Made for Hire claims.
*   **Ruling:** The court allowed the artists' claims to proceed, rejecting UMG's blanket assertion that all sound recordings are WFH. 
*   **Impact:** Demonstrated that legacy recording contracts can be successfully challenged, though class action certification was ultimately denied, requiring individual artists to fight their own battles.

### Scorpio Music S.A. v. Willis (S.D. Cal., 2012)
*   **Issue:** Can a single co-author (Victor Willis of the Village People) unilaterally terminate his share of a grant, or does it require a majority of all co-authors?
*   **Ruling:** The court held that a joint author who separately transfers their copyright interest may unilaterally terminate that grant under § 203.
*   **Impact:** Clarified the rights of individual songwriters within a band or writing team to reclaim their specific shares.

## Practical Applications

If a publisher rejects a client's termination notice claiming the grant only applied to US rights, the agent will immediately cite *Vetter v. Resnik* to argue that the termination effectively recaptured the global copyright, shifting the negotiating leverage back to the client.

## Escalation Requirements

Escalate to litigation counsel if the specific facts of a client's case directly conflict with established precedent in their specific judicial circuit (e.g., the 2nd vs. 9th Circuit), requiring a nuanced legal strategy.
