# Termination Exceptions: Work Made for Hire & Derivative Works

## Overview

This document outlines the two major statutory exceptions to US copyright termination: the Work Made for Hire (WFH) exception and the Derivative Works exception. These are the primary defensive shields used by publishers and labels to defeat termination claims.

## Key Concepts

Not all grants can be terminated. If a work was created as a 'work made for hire,' no termination right exists. Furthermore, even if termination is successful, certain derivative works created prior to termination may continue to be exploited.

## Detailed Information

### The Work Made for Hire (WFH) Exception
*   **Definition:** A work prepared by an employee within the scope of employment, or a work specially ordered or commissioned for use as a contribution to a collective work, part of a motion picture, etc., if the parties expressly agree in a written instrument that the work is a WFH.
*   **Impact:** Works made for hire are completely exempt from termination under both § 203 and § 304. The employer/commissioning party is considered the statutory author.
*   **Industry Practice:** Record labels routinely insert WFH language into recording agreements. The validity of these clauses for sound recordings is heavily litigated (e.g., *Waite v. UMG Recordings*).

### The Derivative Works Exception
*   **Definition:** A derivative work prepared under authority of the grant before its termination may continue to be utilized under the terms of the grant after its termination.
*   **Limitation:** This privilege does not extend to the preparation of *new* derivative works after termination.
*   **Example:** If a book rights grant is terminated, the publisher can no longer print the book, but a movie studio that previously licensed the rights to make a film (a derivative work) can continue to distribute that specific film. 
*   **Music Context:** A publisher whose grant is terminated can continue to collect on pre-existing sync licenses (like a song placed in a TV show), but cannot issue new sync licenses.

## Practical Applications

During catalog forensics, the agent will scrutinize original contracts for WFH language. If WFH language is present in a standard songwriter agreement (where the writer was clearly not an employee), the agent will flag this as a high-risk area requiring legal challenge.

## Escalation Requirements

Escalate to litigation counsel immediately if a publisher formally rejects a Notice of Termination by asserting the works are Works Made for Hire, as this requires a factual analysis of the author's employment status at the time of creation.
